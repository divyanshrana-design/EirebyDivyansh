What is a project?

A project has a clear start and end. Operations don't. Launching a new product is a project. Running the existing line is operations. The chart below shows where each one really differs.

Project vs Operations

How the two differ across six attributes — hover the points for detail.

Bottom line: Projects have an end, an owner, a deliverable and a budget. If your work has all four, you have a project — manage it like one.

The 5 phases of any project

Every project, from a Cork café brunch menu to building the Aviva Stadium, moves through these five phases. The depth changes; the sequence doesn't. Click any phase to expand it.

Initiation — is this worth doing?

The cheapest projects are the ones you kill here. Decide if the idea matches your goals, whether you can do it, and who needs to be involved.

You walk out with
  • A one page project charter
  • A rough budget range
  • Top 3 risks identified
  • A nominated sponsor
Skip this and
  • You commit money before agreement
  • Goalposts move every week
  • The team has no shared "why"
Irish SME example: A Cork café owner wants weekend brunch. Initiation = costing ingredients, checking staffing, confirming the POS handles it, writing 5 lines on "why". 2 hours of work. Saves a month of chaos.

Planning — how will we do it?

Planning is where you find problems while they're still cheap. You don't need 200 pages — you need 5 things, done roughly, and revised weekly.

A usable plan has
  • Work breakdown (tasks & subtasks)
  • A schedule with milestones
  • Resource list (people, money, tools)
  • Comms plan (who hears what, when)
  • Risk register
Watch for
  • Skipping risks "because they're theoretical"
  • Plans nobody on the team has read
  • Treating the plan as fixed forever
Quote: Planning isn't paperwork. It's solving problems before they show up.

Execution — do the work

The longest phase. Most small teams spend 80% of their hours here. Your job shifts from planning to unblocking the team.

You actually do
  • Coordinate people and resources
  • Run the agreed processes
  • Produce the deliverables
  • Handle change requests
  • Celebrate small wins (it matters)
Watch for
  • Team drowning in meetings
  • Heroic individual effort masking process gaps
  • Scope quietly creeping

Monitoring — are we still on track?

Runs in parallel with execution. Think of it as the project's radar (monitoring) and steering wheel (controlling).

You actively check
  • Actual progress vs. the plan
  • Budget and resource use
  • Quality of deliverables
  • Emerging risks early
  • Stakeholder mood
Where AI earns its keep
  • Summarising weekly status
  • Spotting trends in spend
  • Drafting funder updates
Most monitoring work is dull. All of it is handed off to AI beautifully — see the AI playbook.

Closing — wrap it up properly

Most teams skip closing. It costs them every time. A proper close is the highest leverage 30 minutes left in the project.

You finish
  • Formal sign off from stakeholders
  • Admin (contracts, invoices, tools)
  • Handover to operations
  • One page lessons learned note
  • Thank yous to the team
Skip this and
  • Scope creep that never ends
  • Invoices unpaid for months
  • Same mistakes on the next project
  • Stale subscriptions costing you €100s/mo

Where the time actually goes

Typical share of project effort across the five phases (small team baseline).

The surprise: Planning is short, execution is long. Most teams instinctively skip planning to "save time" and pay for it 3× during execution.

Pick a methodology

Why it matters more in 2026

Hybrid project management has overtaken Waterfall. AI has collapsed the cost of project admin. For Irish SMEs and charities, structured PM is no longer a "nice to have" — it's how you win grants and compete with bigger teams.

Hybrid is overtaking Waterfall

Adoption among small and mid sized teams, 2020 to 2026 (estimated 2025/26).

What this means for you: The "purist" approaches are losing. Most real Irish SMEs use a hybrid — Waterfall style planning with Agile or Kanban execution. Pick the right tool for the job, not the religion.

The 13.7 hour problem

Asana's "Anatomy of Work" study: knowledge workers lose 13.7 hours a week to scattered tools, status chasing and context switching.

That's 89 working days a year. Even a free tool like Trello or Notion beats running everything from your inbox.

What a project manager actually does

In a small team there's rarely a dedicated PM. The role is worn by the owner, a senior staffer, or whoever is best organised. Whoever it is, the work is the same — and communication beats every other skill.

Common mistakes to avoid

Skipping planning

"Just start" → missed deadlines and overspend. Plan rough, plan often.

Assuming people understand

Silence isn't agreement. Set comms cadence, channel and format up front.

Treating risk as someone else's job

30 minutes of risk planning saves weeks of firefighting.

Running it from your inbox

13.7 hours a week lost. Even free Trello or Notion beats email + memory.
